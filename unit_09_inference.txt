support;the set of values a random variable can actually take — e.g. a dice roll has support {1,2,3,4,5,6}; a height has support (0, ∞)
compact support;values within a finite range — e.g. Uniform(0,1) has compact support [0,1]
infinite support;values that extend without bound — e.g. Normal distribution: support = (−∞, +∞)
semi-infinite support;bounded in one direction only — e.g. exponential distribution: support = [0, +∞)
probability density function (pdf);describes where probability mass is concentrated for a continuous variable — f(x)≥0, ∫f(x)dx=1; f(x) is a density not a probability, can exceed 1
meaning of density versus probability;P(2 ≤ X ≤ 3) = ∫₂³ f(x) dx; the density f(x) itself is not a probability — it's like kg/m³ vs kg
cumulative distribution function (cdf);F(x) = P(X ≤ x) — always in [0,1], non-decreasing → e.g. for N(0,1): F(0) = 0.5, F(1.96) ≈ 0.975
continuous random variables;can take any real value in a range — height, temperature, time — individual values have probability zero; ranges have positive probability
issues with continuous random variables;P(X = exactly 3.14159...) = 0 — you must ask P(X ∈ interval) instead
uniform distribution;every value in [a,b] equally likely → f(x) = 1/(b-a) for x ∈ [a,b] → e.g. Uniform(0,1): flat PDF, F(x) = x
multivariate distributions;probability distributions over vectors — e.g. a 2-D Gaussian describes joint distribution of height and weight
univariate normal;$\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ — bell curve centred at μ with spread σ → e.g. human heights ~ N(170cm, 100cm²)
properties of the normal distribution;symmetric bell curve; mean = median = mode = μ; 68% of values within ±σ, 95% within ±2σ, 99.7% within ±3σ
multivariate normal;$\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ — generalises the bell curve to D dimensions; mean vector μ sets centre, covariance matrix Σ sets shape and orientation
mean vector;$\boldsymbol{\mu} = E[\mathbf{X}]$ — the centre of a multivariate distribution → e.g. for 2-D Gaussian: $\boldsymbol{\mu} = [\mu_1, \mu_2]$
covariance matrix;$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ — its shape determines the spread and correlation of the distribution → diagonal Σ: axes-aligned ellipse; off-diagonal entries: tilted ellipse
central limit theorem (CLT);sum (or mean) of many independent, identically distributed random variables → approximately normally distributed, regardless of original distribution → e.g. average of 30 dice rolls ≈ N(3.5, 35/36/30)
conditions for CLT;1) independent observations; 2) identically distributed; 3) sample size N sufficiently large (rule of thumb: N ≥ 30)
sampling distribution;the distribution of a statistic (e.g. sample mean $\bar{x}$) over many repeated samples → by CLT: $\bar{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/N)$
population distribution;the true underlying distribution of all individuals — usually unknown; we estimate it from samples
standard error;$\text{SE} = \sigma/\sqrt{N}$ — standard deviation of the sample mean → larger N → smaller SE → more precise estimate
implications of CLT;allows us to build confidence intervals and hypothesis tests for ANY underlying distribution as long as N is large enough
importance in inferential statistics;the CLT is why "assume normality" works for many statistical tests — sums of random variables converge to Gaussian
limitations of CLT;breaks down for very small N; fails for heavy-tailed distributions (e.g. Cauchy); requires i.i.d.
population;the entire set of individuals or measurements of interest — usually too large to measure fully
sample;a subset drawn from the population to make inferences about it → e.g. 1000 randomly selected voters out of millions
random sampling;every possible sample of size N has equal probability of selection — ensures the sample is unbiased
sampling bias;non-random selection causes the sample to misrepresent the population → e.g. surveying only Twitter users about social media habits
representative sample;a sample whose statistics (mean, variance, etc.) closely match the population's
sampling error;the difference between a sample statistic and the population parameter due to chance — unavoidable, decreases as N increases
population parameter;a fixed (usually unknown) property of the whole population → e.g. true mean height μ of all humans
sample statistic;computed from the observed sample → estimates the population parameter → e.g. sample mean $\bar{x}$ estimates μ
inference;using sample statistics to draw conclusions about population parameters — the core task of statistics
generalizability;whether findings from a sample extend to the broader population — depends on random sampling and sample size
varieties of inference; three main approaches: direct (plug-in), maximum likelihood estimation (MLE), Bayesian
direct inference;use sample statistics directly as estimates → e.g. estimate P(heads) by counting observed heads/total flips
maximum likelihood estimation (MLE);choose θ that makes the observed data most probable: $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_\theta \prod_i P(x_i|\theta)$ or equivalently minimise negative log-likelihood
likelihood function;$L(\theta|\text{data}) = P(\text{data}|\theta)$ — viewed as a function of θ with the data fixed; MLE finds its peak
Bayesian inference;combine prior P(θ) with likelihood P(data|θ) via Bayes: $P(\theta|\text{data}) \propto P(\text{data}|\theta)\,P(\theta)$ → produces a full posterior distribution over θ
prior probability;P(θ) — your belief about θ before seeing data → e.g. assume coin bias ~ Uniform(0,1) before any flips
likelihood;P(data|θ) — how probable the observed data is given specific θ
posterior probability;P(θ|data) — belief about θ after observing data; combines prior and likelihood via Bayes' rule
Bayes' theorem;$P(\theta|\text{data}) = \dfrac{P(\text{data}|\theta)\,P(\theta)}{P(\text{data})}$ — updates the prior using the likelihood of the observed data
advantages of Bayesian inference;uses prior knowledge; gives full uncertainty distribution over θ; works with small samples; natural model comparison
disadvantages of Bayesian inference;requires specifying a prior; computationally expensive (often needs MCMC); subjective choice of prior
probabilistic programming;write models as code with random variables → inference engine (e.g. MCMC, variational inference) runs automatically → e.g. PyMC, Stan
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC);draws samples from the posterior by simulating a Markov chain that has the posterior as its stationary distribution — used when the posterior has no closed form
MCMC algorithm steps;1. Start at an initial θ. 2. Propose a new θ' from a proposal distribution. 3. Accept θ' with probability min(1, P(θ'|data)/P(θ|data)). 4. Repeat → build up a trace of samples from the posterior.
Metropolis-Hastings algorithm;a specific MCMC method using a symmetric proposal distribution — accepts/rejects proposed moves to ensure samples come from the posterior
Burn-in period;initial MCMC samples before the chain has converged — discarded because they depend on the (arbitrary) starting point → e.g. discard first 1000 samples
thinning; keeping only every k-th MCMC sample to reduce autocorrelation between consecutive samples → e.g. keep sample 1000, 2000, 3000, ...
convergence diagnostics;tests to check if the MCMC chain has reached the target distribution → e.g. trace plots should look like "fuzzy caterpillars", R-hat ≈ 1
probabilistic programming language;a language for specifying probabilistic models — the inference is handled automatically → e.g. PyMC, Stan, Pyro
advantages of probabilistic programming;rapid model prototyping; easy to change the model; supports complex hierarchical and nonparametric models
limitations of MCMC;computationally slow; may need many iterations to converge; sensitive to the proposal distribution and initial conditions
posterior distribution; P(θ|data) — the full distribution of beliefs about θ after observing data — summarises uncertainty in the parameters
posterior predictive; distribution of future observations given past data: $P(x_\text{new}|\text{data}) = \int P(x_\text{new}|\theta)\,P(\theta|\text{data})\,d\theta$
prior predictive; distribution of data before observing anything: $P(x) = \int P(x|\theta)\,P(\theta)\,d\theta$ — what the model expects before training
linear regression by direct estimation; minimise $\sum_i (y_i - \theta^T x_i)^2$ → closed form solution $\hat{\theta} = (X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty = X^+ y$ via pseudo-inverse
linear regression by maximum likelihood estimation; assume $y_i = \theta^T x_i + \epsilon_i$ with $\epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ → maximising the likelihood gives the same solution as minimising squared error
linear regression by Bayesian inference; place a prior on θ (e.g. $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2 I)$) → posterior $P(\theta|\text{data})$ is also Gaussian → gives uncertainty estimates on predictions
What is the difference between a population parameter and a sample statistic?;parameter: fixed property of the whole population (usually unknown); statistic: computed from the sample and used to estimate the parameter
What is an estimator?;a function of observed samples that produces an estimate of a population parameter → e.g. $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{N}\sum x_i$ estimates the population mean μ
What is the sample mean as an estimator?;$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{N}\sum x_i$ — unbiased (E[$\bar{x}$] = μ); standard error = σ/√N; converges to μ as N→∞
What is the sample variance as an estimator?;$s^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2$ estimates population variance σ²; often use N−1 in denominator (Bessel's correction) for unbiased estimate
What is maximum likelihood estimation (MLE)?;find $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_\theta \log L(\theta|\text{data}) = \arg\max_\theta \sum_i \log P(x_i|\theta)$ — find parameters that make the observed data most probable
What is the score function in MLE?;$u(\theta) = \nabla_\theta \log L(\theta|\text{data})$ — the gradient of the log-likelihood; set to zero to find MLE; can be optimised by gradient descent
What is the iid assumption?;observations are Independent and Identically Distributed → independence: knowing one tells you nothing about others; identical: all drawn from same P(x|θ) → makes likelihood a product: $\prod_i P(x_i|\theta)$
What is the predictive posterior?;$P(x_\text{new}|\text{data}) = \int P(x_\text{new}|\theta)\,P(\theta|\text{data})\,d\theta$ — marginalises over uncertainty in θ; gives calibrated uncertainty in predictions
What is a conjugate prior?;a prior that when combined with a specific likelihood produces a posterior of the same distributional family → e.g. Beta prior + Binomial likelihood = Beta posterior; allows closed-form Bayesian updates
What is variational inference?;approximate the posterior with a tractable distribution Q(θ) by minimising KL divergence D(Q||P) — faster than MCMC but approximate
What is the burn-in period in MCMC?;initial samples discarded because the chain hasn't reached its stationary distribution yet — starting point biases early samples
What is thinning in MCMC?;keep only every k-th sample — reduces autocorrelation between consecutive samples so they behave more like independent draws from the posterior
What does the trace in MCMC represent?;the sequence of θ values sampled — its histogram approximates the posterior; a good trace looks like a "fuzzy caterpillar" (well-mixed, no drift)
What assumption does ordinary linear regression make about errors?;$\epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ — errors are normally distributed with constant variance and zero mean; this is equivalent to maximising Gaussian likelihood
What does Bayesian linear regression infer compared to MLE?;MLE gives a single point estimate $\hat{\theta}$; Bayesian gives a full distribution $P(\theta|\text{data})$ — uncertainty grows in data-sparse regions
What is the multivariate normal PDF?;$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{Z}\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$ — the exponent measures the Mahalanobis distance from the mean
How do you sample from a multivariate normal?;draw D independent N(0,1) samples into n, then $\mathbf{x} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{1/2}\mathbf{n} + \boldsymbol{\mu}$ — the square root of Σ "shapes" the noise into the right covariance
What is the CDF and how does it differ from the PDF?;CDF: $F(x) = P(X \leq x)$ — a true probability in [0,1]; PDF: $f(x)$ — a density, can exceed 1, must be integrated over a range to give a probability
